# Antaeus Design System — Motion

**Status:** DRAFT for founder review.
**Date:** 2026-06-07.
**Author:** Claude, working session with the founder (mrcoe7@gmail.com).
**Branch:** `claude/antaeus-gtmos-design-system`.
**Scope:** This is the eighth sibling document under the design system charter (`00-charter-2026-06-02.md`). It operationalizes the canon's motion doctrine (Part II §6 — "sparse, consequential, state-based; it guides attention, it does not entertain") into the actual grammar a build uses: the duration and easing tokens, the named motions the product is allowed to perform, the reduced-motion floor the component library already mandates (`03` §4.9), and the rule that motion is never load-bearing for meaning. The charter named motion as an owed sibling; this is it. It is deliberately small, because a motion system that offers many motions is a motion system that will be overrun.

---

## 0. Why this exists

The canon already states the doctrine and the component library already requires `prefers-reduced-motion` on every animation. What neither provides is the grammar — how long a transition runs, what curve it follows, which specific motions the product performs and which it refuses, what happens when the operator switches reads or a band empties or a state settles. Without that grammar, motion gets added per-component by whoever is building, in whatever duration felt right that day, and the product accumulates the microinteraction excess and hover theater the canon forbids one tween at a time. The today-surface mockup already gestured at motion it did not specify — the pulse shimmer, the read switch, the Wayfinder's inline grow — and those need a single definition before they are built.

This spec resolves the grammar. It commits to a four-step duration scale and two easings; a closed vocabulary of named motions, each tied to a state change rather than to decoration; the reduced-motion floor as a hard requirement; and the rule that no meaning ever rides on motion, so a surface with motion turned off still says everything it said with motion on. Motion is the fourth orthogonal axis, after voice, density, and layout: it changes *how a change is shown*, never what is shown.

---

## Part I — What motion is for

Motion in Antaeus does exactly one job: it makes a state change legible. When something becomes true — a surface loads, a read switches, a save lands, a band empties, a deal tips into real risk — motion shows the *transition* so the operator's eye follows the change instead of hunting for what moved. That is the whole permitted use.

Everything outside that job is banned, and the canon is explicit (Part II §6): no hover theater, no microinteraction excess, no friendliness. A button does not bounce. An icon does not wiggle on hover. A card does not animate for delight. The product is severe and instrument-like; an instrument's needle moves because a value changed, not to be charming. If a motion is not showing a state change, it does not ship.

Two consequences follow. First, motion is **sparse** — most interactions have none, and the few that do are the ones where a state genuinely changed. Second, motion is **never load-bearing** — because the operator may have it turned off (Part IV), every meaning a motion helps convey is also conveyed without it. Motion clarifies; it is never the only thing carrying the read.

## Part II — The tokens

### 2.1 Durations

Four steps, and a build that reaches for a fifth is usually entertaining rather than clarifying:

- **Instant — 0ms.** No animation. The default for most interactions and the entirety of the reduced-motion state.
- **Quick — 120ms.** The smallest perceptible transition. State-change confirmations, focus rings settling, a toggle flipping.
- **Base — 200ms.** The standard transition. A read switch, the Wayfinder's inline grow, a band collapse.
- **Considered — 320ms.** The longest the product allows, reserved for first-load staging and the rare set-piece (the Readiness verdict-transition ceremony in Founding GTM, canon §4.19). Nothing runs longer than 320ms; a transition the operator has to wait on has stopped clarifying and started performing.

### 2.2 Easings

Two curves, both decelerating, neither playful:

- **Standard (ease-out, `cubic-bezier(0.2, 0, 0, 1)`).** Things entering and settling decelerate into place. This is the default for nearly everything — it reads as confident and calm, the motion equivalent of the product's voice.
- **Exit (ease-in, `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 1, 1)`).** Things leaving accelerate away. Used only when something dismisses or collapses.

There is no bounce, no overshoot, no elastic, no spring. Those curves read as friendly, and friendly is the register the charter spends its whole length avoiding. The severity of the product is in its motion curves as much as its color.

### 2.3 Staging

The one composite token: **stagger — 40ms per item**, used only in first-load staging (§3.1), capped so a region never takes longer than the Considered duration to fully arrive regardless of item count. Stagger is the only place multiple elements animate in sequence; everywhere else, a single element transitions alone.

## Part III — The motion vocabulary

A closed list. These are the motions the product performs; anything not on this list is not a motion the product has.

- **First-load staging.** When a surface first composes — on initial app load or first entry to a room — its regions arrive in their stack order (`05` §2.2) with the 40ms stagger, on Standard easing, within the Considered duration. This happens **once**, not on every navigation; returning to a surface the operator has seen does not re-stage it. Staging is how the system shows it has composed a page rather than dumped one.
- **The read switch.** When the operator switches between Brief, Spotlight, and Queue (`04` §3.2), the read in the focal slot cross-fades and swaps on the Base duration; the lower bands do not move (`04` §3.6 — they are furniture, not the read). The switch shows that the *same* ranked work is being re-lensed, not replaced.
- **The inline grow.** When the operator clicks "Why" on the Wayfinder (`03` §3.2), the disclosure grows downward in place — height and opacity, Base duration, Standard easing. It grows rather than appears so the eye understands it expanded from the bar rather than covering the page. Closing reverses it on the Exit curve.
- **State-change confirmation.** When a save lands, an outcome is logged, or a gauge changes color because an object's state changed, the change settles on the Quick duration. A save's toast enters on Standard; the gauge's new color crossfades rather than snapping, so the operator catches that the state moved. This is canon Part III §3 rule 5 (every save must visibly matter) rendered as the smallest honest motion.
- **Region collapse.** When a band empties — no observations, no proposal waiting (`04` §3.6, `05` §2.2) — it collapses its height to zero on the Exit curve, Base duration, and the rhythm closes over it. Collapse shows the region left rather than was never there.
- **The pulse.** The single ambient, looping animation in the product: the most-pressured pulse dot on the Pulse timeline shimmers (`03` §3.1, the today-surface Queue). It is the one exception to "motion shows a transition" — it is a standing emphasis on the single most-pressured object, and it is the most-watched candidate for overuse, so it is restricted to exactly one dot per surface and gated hard by reduced motion. No other element loops, ever.
- **Escalation.** Reserved for real, destructive risk (canon Part III §3 rule 7) — a deal about to close-lost, an unsaved edit about to be discarded. Escalation may use a brief, sharper motion to pull attention, and it is the only place the product is permitted to be insistent. It is never used for ordinary ranking or to drive engagement; an escalation motion that fires on a non-destructive event is a bug.
- **Continuity across rooms.** When the operator moves between rooms (`canon §6` continuity), the Wayfinder bar persists — it does not flash, reload, or re-stage; its *here* and *pulling* cells update on the Quick duration while the bar itself stays put. The persistence is the point: the bar staying still while the room changes beneath it is how the system shows context carried.

That is the entire vocabulary. There is no hover motion, no loading spinner that outlives the skeleton (`03` §4.4 holds the shape instead), no decorative entrance on cards beyond first-load staging, no animated number counters, no parallax.

## Part IV — Reduced motion and the contract

### 4.1 The reduced-motion floor

Every motion in Part III is gated behind `prefers-reduced-motion` (`03` §4.9). When the operator has reduced motion set, the product resolves every state change **instantly**: no staging stagger, no read-switch crossfade, no inline grow, no gauge crossfade, no collapse animation, and the pulse shimmer does not run. The surface arrives, switches, expands, and settles in its target state on the next paint. Because motion is never load-bearing (Part I), nothing is lost — the operator with reduced motion reads exactly what the operator with motion reads, just without the transitions. This is a floor, not a feature: a motion that breaks meaning when removed has violated the spec.

### 4.2 The contract

A component or surface that animates declares two things: the **named motion** it performs (one of Part III — it does not invent a motion) and the **token** it uses (a Part II duration and easing — it does not pick an arbitrary number). A motion that is not in the vocabulary, or a duration that is not on the scale, is drift the same way an off-scale spacing value or an un-banned word is. The build can check both: the motion against the vocabulary, the duration against the scale.

### 4.3 What this spec does not decide

It does not decide the **ceremony set-pieces** in depth — the Readiness-up verdict transition that fires the Founding GTM moment (canon §4.19) is named here as the one place the Considered duration stretches toward a designed moment, but its choreography is owned by that room's build, within these tokens. It does not re-decide the doctrine (canon Part II §6) or the reduced-motion requirement (`03` §4.9); it operationalizes them. And it does not specify sound or haptics, because the product has neither.

## Part V — Migration, citations, signals

### 5.1 Migration of the existing rooms

The migrated rooms carry whatever transitions their original builds happened to include. Migration to this grammar is a subtraction more than an addition: most rooms have *too much* incidental motion (hover transitions, decorative fades) rather than too little, and the retrofit removes everything not in the Part III vocabulary and re-times what remains to the Part II tokens. It rides along with each room's component-library retrofit (`03` §5.1). The Dashboard is first because it is the first build, and its read switch, inline grow, and single pulse are the vocabulary's first real exercise.

### 5.2 Citations

Canon Part II §6 (the motion doctrine), Part III §3 rule 5 (every save must visibly matter), rule 7 (escalation beats clutter), §4.19 (the Founding GTM ceremony). Component library `03` §4.4 (loading skeletons hold the shape), §4.9 (reduced-motion floor), §3.1–§3.2 (the pulse, the Wayfinder grow). Today surface `04` §3.2 (read switch), §3.6 (collapsing bands). Layout `05` §2.2 (the region stack staging follows).

### 5.3 Signals the spec is doing its job

1. **Motion only shows transitions.** An audit finds no hover theater, no decorative entrances, no animated counters — every motion in the product maps to a state change.
2. **Nothing runs long.** No transition exceeds the Considered duration; the product never makes the operator wait on an animation.
3. **One loop, one dot.** Exactly one ambient animation exists — the single most-pressured pulse — and nothing else loops.
4. **Reduced motion loses nothing.** With motion off, every surface reads identically; no meaning was riding on a transition.
5. **The vocabulary is closed.** New rooms perform only the named motions at the named tokens; none invents a motion or an off-scale duration.

## Closing

Motion is the smallest of the orthogonal axes and the easiest to ruin. A product comes by its calm partly through what it refuses to animate — the still button, the un-bouncing icon, the surface that simply arrives in its target state. Antaeus performs a closed handful of motions, each tied to a state genuinely changing, each on a short decelerating curve with no playfulness in it, each removable without losing a word of meaning. The needle moves because the value moved. With motion specified, the product has its fourth axis: voice says what, density says how much, layout says where, and motion says how the change is shown — four axes that never have to re-learn each other. The remaining sibling is iconography.
